History

Malaysia's Foreign Policy: Evolution and Development

Malaysia's foreign policy has undergone several significant developments since its independence in 1957. Initially, Malaysia was an active member of international organizations such as the Commonwealth of Nations and the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), and it opposed apartheid in South Africa. In the late 1960s, the country shifted its policy towards non-alignment and neutrality, emphasizing peaceful relations regardless of other countries' ideologies. Malaysia continues to pursue an independent, principled, and pragmatic foreign policy, founded on the values of peace, humanity, justice, and equality. In 2019, the Pakatan Harapan administration released the Foreign Policy Framework of the New Malaysia, which emphasized continuity alongside new reformative changes in approach and spotlighted certain issues. This history reflects Malaysia's commitment to maintaining peaceful relations, regional cooperation, and a principled approach to international affairs.

Unveiling the Latest in Foreign Policy

The mainstay of Malaysia's foreign policy remains

The main principles of Malaysia's foreign policy such as the exclusion policy and cooperation with other countries will continue to be maintained, said the new Foreign Minister Senator Datuk Seri Zambry Abdul Kadir.

Analyst: Malaysia needs to set its foreign policy clearly and urgently

New Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim has yet to announce a foreign policy, with analysts arguing that the move is urgently needed to show how Malaysia can protect national interests while navigating the US-China rivalry in the region.

Have friendly Malaysia-China relations gone awry?

For now, Malaysia remains committed to maintaining public displays of friendly relations with China while handling any differences quietly. But the difficulties and pressures of keeping the friendship real, alive, and substantive are greater than ever.

How will Malaysia's Foreign Policy under Anwar Ibrahim play out?

While Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim's recent foreign overseas trips have produced no shortage of headlines, they have also raised a broader question about how Malaysia's foreign policy may play out under his leadership. This dynamic bears careful watching, both due to Malaysia’s own regional and global significance as well as wider trends at play in the Indo-Pacific region and the international system more generally.
